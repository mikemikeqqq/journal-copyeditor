# Academic Critical Style

Use this reference for manuscript editing, reviewer-facing comments, in-document reports, and response letters.

## Voice

Use formal academic English with clear, common vocabulary. Be direct, critical, and professional.

Avoid:

- flattery;
- vague reassurance;
- AI-style phrasing;
- long conclusions;
- formulaic endings;
- em dashes;
- uncommon words where common words are precise;
- repetition and redundancy;
- unsupported absolute claims.

Pair criticism with concrete revision solutions. Do not simply say that a claim is weak; state how to revise it.

## Critical Focus

Identify weaknesses in:

- theory;
- logic;
- methods;
- contribution;
- boundary conditions;
- falsifiability;
- identification;
- overlap with prior research.

For each weakness, give a specific fix, such as narrowing a claim, adding a boundary condition, clarifying a construct, adding a citation, revising a hypothesis rationale, or moving an unsupported implication to a limitation.

## Construct Consistency

Maintain strict construct consistency.

- Use the same term for the same construct throughout a manuscript or response.
- Do not introduce constructs that are not measured in the study.
- Do not replace the author's constructs with broader, narrower, or more fashionable terms unless explicitly requested.
- Do not import terminology from cited studies if it conflicts with the manuscript's focal constructs.
- Adapt cited evidence to the manuscript's conceptual language while preserving accuracy.

This rule is especially important in:

- introduction;
- hypothesis development;
- discussion;
- theoretical implications;
- managerial implications;
- response letters.

If the manuscript uses inconsistent labels for the same construct, standardize the term only when the intended construct is clear. Otherwise, add a comment or report item asking the author to choose the construct label.

## Methodological Language

Be careful with methodological language.

- Do not use causal terms unless the research design justifies causal inference.
- Treat correlations, regression coefficients, meta-analytic estimates, structural paths, and moderation results as associations unless the design supports causal interpretation.
- Use causal language only for experiments, quasi-experiments, longitudinal identification strategies, or other credible causal designs, and explain why the claim is justified.
- Do not infer significance, mediation, moderation, robustness, or theoretical support beyond the reported evidence.

Safer language:

- `is associated with`;
- `is related to`;
- `predicts` only when the design and model support predictive wording;
- `is consistent with`;
- `provides evidence for an association`;
- `the results support the proposed relationship` when the test is reported.

Avoid unless justified:

- `causes`;
- `leads to`;
- `drives`;
- `determines`;
- `proves`;
- `demonstrates` for causal or theoretical claims that only have associational evidence.

## Literature and Evidence

Support key claims with authentic literature, sound theory, or verifiable facts.

- Use correct citations and reference lists when requested.
- Do not fabricate sources, findings, journal rankings, data, effect sizes, or page numbers.
- Prioritize leading journals in the relevant field, especially ABS 3/4 journals where appropriate.
- If available literature does not meet that standard, state this clearly and weaken the claim.
- Match cited evidence to the construct and claim.
- Do not cite studies on one construct as evidence for another unless the conceptual bridge is explicit and defensible.

If a claim lacks support, either add a verified citation supplied by the user, soften the claim, or flag it in the report.

## Manuscript-Ready Revision

When revising academic writing, produce manuscript-ready text rather than explanatory commentary unless the user asks for explanations.

Keep revisions concise when concision is requested, but do not remove:

- theoretical logic;
- construct precision;
- necessary methodological caveats;
- reviewer-facing defensibility;
- alignment between theory, hypotheses, results, and implications.

Avoid superficial revision. Improve argument structure, theoretical sharpness, citation placement, terminology consistency, and alignment across the manuscript.

## Reviewer Response Letters

Use a polite and professional tone.

Each response should:

1. begin with appreciation;
2. state what was changed;
3. state where it was changed;
4. explain how the revision addresses the concern.

Do not overstate revisions. Do not mention changes that were not made. Clearly identify unresolved issues if any remain.

## Manuscript Sections

Write with publishability in mind.

### Introduction

Build a clear research puzzle, define the gap, and avoid unsupported novelty claims.

### Hypothesis Development

Explain:

- why a relationship is expected;
- why the direction is justified;
- why competing explanations are less suitable.

Do not introduce unmeasured constructs to make the theory sound richer.

### Results

Report findings accurately. Do not overstate significance, mediation, moderation, robustness, or support.

### Discussion and Implications

Interpret only tested constructs and reported results.

Do not turn speculative mechanisms, unmeasured constructs, or broad managerial implications into conclusions.

## Report and Comment Style

In the `Copy Editing and Proofreading Report`, use direct issue-solution pairs:

- `Issue: The introduction shifts from product self-expressiveness to symbolic consumption, but symbolic consumption is not measured.`
- `Revision solution: Keep product self-expressiveness as the construct and use symbolic meaning only as a theoretical rationale if cited and clearly linked.`

In Word comments, be short:

- `Please confirm whether this causal wording is justified by the design.`
- `This term appears broader than the measured construct. Please confirm the intended construct label.`
- `Please add a citation that directly supports this claim or weaken the claim.`
