# Style and Argument

Use this reference when sentence polishing alone is not enough. It adapts the strongest patterns from Nature-style manuscript guidance and academic writing workflows for tracked-change copyediting.

## Priority Order

1. Factual fidelity
2. Claim-evidence-boundary
3. Section job
4. Paragraph flow
5. Sentence clarity
6. Rhythm and word choice

Do not make prose smoother by hiding a missing premise, unsupported claim, weak comparator, or ambiguous scope.

## Claim-Evidence-Boundary Check

For every important scientific or scholarly claim, identify:

- `claim`: what the paper says;
- `evidence`: where the manuscript supports it;
- `boundary`: where the claim stops.

Typical copyediting responses:

- claim without evidence: soften the claim and add a comment;
- data without a point: make the topic sentence claim-first if the evidence is clear;
- implication without boundary: add hedging or a scope phrase;
- correlation written as mechanism: change to `is associated with`, `may reflect`, or `is consistent with`, and comment if the intended causal claim is unclear.

## Section Jobs

| Section | Copyediting goal | Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Abstract | Mini-paper: context, gap/objective, approach, key result, implication | Overclaiming, vague importance, missing quantitative detail when supplied |
| Introduction | Move from field context to unresolved gap to present study | Results-summary drift, inflated novelty, too many definitions before the gap |
| Literature Review | Synthesize topics and positions, not a paper-by-paper list | Flattening earlier work to manufacture novelty |
| Methods | Make reproducibility readable without changing procedures | Inventing missing details, polishing away necessary specificity |
| Results | Report what was observed under what conditions | Discussion-style mechanism claims without evidence |
| Discussion | Interpret findings, relate to prior work, state limits | Repeating Results figure by figure, universal conclusions |
| Conclusion | Contribution, decisive evidence, implication with boundary | New data or unsupported promises |

## Paragraph Flow

Use reverse outlining for unclear sections:

1. Write the section thesis in one sentence.
2. Identify each paragraph's message.
3. Check whether each topic sentence supports the section thesis.
4. Check whether every supporting sentence connects by cause, contrast, consequence, refinement, or example.
5. Split, move, or comment on any paragraph that carries two messages.

Strong paragraph openings usually state the point first. Exceptions include brief setup sentences in introductions, transitions, or methods sequences.

## Discipline Voice

- Hard sciences and medicine: prioritize measurement precision, reporting standards, and cautious interpretation.
- Engineering and computer science: preserve specifications, baselines, complexity, datasets, metrics, and reproducibility details.
- Social sciences and education: preserve constructs, participant language, theoretical framing, limitations, and ethical nuance.
- Humanities: preserve argumentative voice, interpretive nuance, source attributions, and theoretical vocabulary.

Do not force one discipline's voice onto another.

## Chinese-Influenced English

When prose appears translated or strongly influenced by Chinese syntax:

- translate intent, not clause order;
- split long sentences into claim, evidence, condition, comparison, implication, and limitation;
- move the gap before the method when the draft lists methods before motivation;
- name the comparator when the draft says something "significantly improves";
- bound `first`, `novel`, `innovative`, and similar claims;
- keep key technical terms stable instead of cycling synonyms.

For mixed Chinese-English manuscripts, preserve intentional Chinese text but verify that new CJK characters were not introduced by corruption.

## Prose Quality Sweep

Fix silently when safe:

- `in order to` -> `to`;
- `due to the fact that` -> `because`;
- `has the ability to` -> `can`;
- `with regard to` -> `regarding`;
- throat-clearing such as `it is important to note that`;
- vague `this` or `it` without a clear antecedent;
- repeated em dashes where commas, parentheses, or a new sentence would be cleaner.

Flag or reconsider, rather than blindly replacing, AI-typical or inflated terms: `delve`, `pivotal`, `crucial`, `showcase`, `realm`, `robust`, `holistic`, `groundbreaking`, `landscape`, `leverage`. These can be valid technical terms in context.
